Extremum Seeking Control (ESC) is a model-free optimization method.  
It drives a system to an unknown optimal input $u^*$ that minimizes a cost function $J(u)$, without requiring the gradient.

## 🔍 Basic Idea

- Perturb the input $u$ with a small sinusoidal signal.
- Observe the effect on $J(u)$.
- Demodulate the output to extract gradient direction.
- Integrate to update $u$.

## 📐 Key Formulas

Let the unknown optimum be $u^*$, input error:

$$
\tilde{u} = u - u^*
$$

Perturbed input:

$$
u(t) = \hat{u}(t) + a \sin(\omega t)
$$

Cost function (Taylor expansion around $u^*$):

$$
J(t) = J(u^*) + \frac{1}{2} J''(u^*) \tilde{u}^2(t) + \dots
$$

After **high-pass filter** → remove DC component.  
Multiply by **demodulation signal** $\sin(\omega t)$.  
After **low-pass filter** + integrator:

$$
\dot{\hat{u}} = k \cdot J''(u^*) \cdot \tilde{u}
$$

This leads to:

$$
\dot{\tilde{u}} = -k J''(u^*) \tilde{u}
$$

Thus **exponential convergence** to $u^*$ if $J''(u^*) > 0$.

## ⚙️ Practical Additions

- **High-pass filter**: removes low-frequency disturbances.
- **Low-pass filter**: removes higher harmonics from demodulation.
- The **dither frequency** $\omega$ affects convergence speed and steady-state accuracy (trade-off with filtering).

## ✅ Advantages

- No model needed.
- Works on real systems.
- Simple to implement.

## ❌ Limitations

- Slow compared to model-based methods.
- Small steady-state oscillation remains.
- Sensitive to tuning (amplitude & frequency).
